Introduction

The Periodic Table

A tabular arrangement of elements in groups (vertical columns) and periods (horizontal rows) highlighting the regular trends in properties of elements.

What is an Element?

Elements are pure substances made up of one type of atoms. They are the basic units of all types of matter. In order to study elements in an organised manner, they need to be classified.

Historical Development of the Periodic Table
Döbereiner — Law of Triads
Grouped elements in sets of three (triads) based on atomic mass similarities. The middle element's atomic mass was approximately the average of the other two.
Newland — Law of Octaves
When elements are arranged in increasing order of their atomic mass, every eighth element beginning from any element resembles the first element in its physical and chemical properties. Limited to 56 elements.
Dmitri Mendeleev — First Periodic Table (1869)
"The properties of elements are the periodic functions of their atomic masses." Arranged 63 known elements in vertical columns (groups) and horizontal rows (periods). Predicted existence of undiscovered elements. Limitation: Could not explain positions of rare earth metals and isotopes.
Henry Moseley — Modern Periodic Law
"The physical and chemical properties of elements are the periodic functions of their atomic number." This resolved all defects of Mendeleev's table. Niel Bohr later gave the extended long form of the modern periodic table.
Modern Periodic Law — Henry Moseley
The physical and chemical properties of elements are the periodic functions of their atomic number.

Groups of the Periodic Table

The modern periodic table has eighteen vertical columns known as groups, arranged from left to right.

All 18 Groups at a Glance
🔥
Group 1 — Alkali Metals
Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr. Form strong alkalis with water. One valence electron. Highly reactive metals. Metallic character increases going down the group.
🌍
Group 2 — Alkaline Earth Metals
Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra. Form weaker alkalis compared to Group 1. Two valence electrons. Good reducing agents.
⚙️
Groups 3–12 — Transition Elements
d-block elements. Two outermost shells incomplete. Include Fe, Cu, Zn, Ni, Co. Variable valency. Good conductors.
🅱️
Group 13 — Boron Family
B, Al, Ga, In, Tl. Boron is the first member and a metalloid. Three valence electrons. Al is most widely used metal.
💎
Group 14 — Carbon Family
C, Si, Ge, Sn, Pb. Four valence electrons. Carbon shows catenation. Silicon is a semiconductor widely used in electronics.
💨
Group 15 — Nitrogen Family
N, P, As, Sb, Bi. Five valence electrons. N₂ forms 78% of air. Essential for biological molecules like DNA.
⛏️
Group 16 — Chalcogens
O, S, Se, Te, Po. "Ore-forming" elements. Six valence electrons. Oxygen is most abundant element on Earth's crust.
🧂
Group 17 — Halogens
F, Cl, Br, I, At. "Salt formers". Seven valence electrons. Highly reactive non-metals. Strong oxidising agents. Diatomic molecules.
🫧
Group 18 — Noble Gases (Zero Group)
He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn. Inert gases. Outermost shell is complete. Extremely stable electronic configuration. Barely react.
Main Group / Representative Elements: Groups 1, 2, 13, 14, 15, 16 and 17 are known as main group elements, representative elements, or normal elements. The outermost shell of all these elements is incomplete. Group 18 (noble gases) is the exception — their outermost shell is complete.
S, P, D, F Blocks
s-block
Groups 1 & 2. Last electron enters the s-orbital. Includes alkali metals and alkaline earth metals.
p-block
Groups 13–18. Last electron enters the p-orbital. Includes non-metals, metalloids, and noble gases.
d-block
Groups 3–12. Last electron enters the d-orbital. Transition elements. Two outermost shells incomplete.
f-block
Lanthanides & Actinides. Inner transition elements. Last electron enters the f-orbital. Shown separately below main table.
Typical Elements: The third period elements — Na, Mg, Al, Si, P, S and Cl — summarise the properties of their respective groups and are called typical elements.

Valency

Valency denotes the combining capacity of the atom of an element. It is equal to the number of electrons an atom can donate, accept, or share.

On moving down a given group, the number of electrons in the outermost shell (valence electrons) remains the same. Therefore, valency in a group remains the same. Across a period, valency increases up to Group 14 (valency = 4), then decreases — in Group 17 it becomes 1.
Valency Rule:
• If outer shell electrons = 1, 2, 3, or 4 → Valency = those electrons
• If outer shell electrons = 5, 6, or 7 → Valency = 8 − outer electrons
• Example: N has 5 outer electrons → Valency = 8 − 5 = 3
• Valency is always positive (Note 2).

Shells (Orbits)

Electrons revolve around the nucleus in certain definite circular paths called orbits or shells (designated K, L, M, N, O, P from innermost to outermost).

Number of shells in an element = Period number to which that element belongs.

Across a Period — Shells Stay the Same

In the 2nd period, the number of shells = 2 for all elements (Li → Ne). In the 3rd period, the number of shells = 3 for all elements (Na → Ar) and so on.

Core Concept

Periodicity of Elements

Properties that reappear at regular intervals or show gradual variation at regular intervals are called periodic properties.

Definition — Periodicity
The properties that reappear at regular intervals, or in which there is gradual variation (i.e. increase or decrease) at regular intervals, are called 'periodic properties' and the phenomenon is known as the periodicity of elements.
Cause of Periodicity
Root Cause
The cause of periodicity is the recurrence of similar electronic configuration i.e. having the same number of electrons in the outermost orbit.

In a particular group, electrons in the outermost orbit remain the same — i.e. electronic configuration is similar. Since chemical properties of elements depend upon the number of electrons in their outermost shell, elements of the same group have similar properties.

Example — Group 17 (Halogens)

All halogens have 7 electrons in their outermost shells. They share these common properties:

Coloured non-metals Form negative ions (Cl⁻) Very reactive Found in combined state Good oxidising agents Slightly soluble in water M.P. & B.P. increase down group Dissolve better in organic solvents

Periodic Property 1

Atomic Size (Atomic Radius)

It is the distance between the centre of the nucleus of an atom and its outermost shell.

Definition
Atomic radius = distance from nucleus centre to outermost shell. Also defined as half the inter-nuclear distance between combined atoms in a molecule.
Units of Atomic Size

Angstrom (Å):  1 Å = 10⁻¹⁰ m

Picometre (pm):  1 pm = 10⁻¹² m

Atomic Size Depends On
Number of Shells

An increase in the number of shells increases the size of an atom because the distance between the outermost shell and the nucleus increases.

Nuclear Charge

An increase in nuclear charge decreases the size of the atom because electrons are attracted towards the nucleus with a greater force, bringing the outermost shell closer.

Trends in Atomic Size
↓ Down a Group — Atomic Size INCREASES
Atomic Size — Down a Group↑ Increases

Reason: Addition of new shells overweighs the increased nuclear charge. As one moves from one period to the next in a group, successive shells are added.

Special Cases — Ions vs Atoms
📌 Note 1: Cation is ALWAYS SMALLER than its parent atom ▼

Reason: Cation is formed by the loss of electron(s). After losing electrons, protons > electrons. Electrons are more strongly attracted by the nucleus and are pulled inward. Hence the size decreases.

Na (2,8,1) − e⁻ → Na⁺ (2,8) [Na⁺ is smaller than Na]
Mg (2,8,2) − 2e⁻ → Mg²⁺ (2,8) [Mg²⁺ is smaller than Mg]
📌 Note 2: Anion is ALWAYS LARGER than its parent atom ▼

Reason: Anion is formed by the gain of electron(s). Thus electrons > protons. The effective positive charge in the nucleus is less, so less inward pull is experienced. Hence the size expands.

Cl (2,8,7) + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (2,8,8) [Cl⁻ is larger than Cl]
O (2,6) + 2e⁻ → O²⁻ (2,8) [O²⁻ is larger than O]
📌 Note 3: Isoelectronic Ions — same electrons, different sizes ▼

Ions having the same number of electrons are called isoelectronic ions. Their size depends on the nuclear charge (number of protons). Greater the nuclear charge, smaller the size.

📌 Note 4: Inert Gas Atomic Size Exception ▼

The size of inert gas atoms is bigger than expected (bigger than the preceding halogen in the same period). This is because the outer shell is complete with the maximum number of electrons. The electronic repulsions are maximum. Hence the size of inert gas atoms is bigger.

Example: Argon (Ar) atom is bigger than Chlorine (Cl) atom, even though Cl has a smaller atomic number.

Periodic Property 2

Metallic Character

Those elements which have a tendency to lose their valence electrons and form a positive ion are considered metals.

Definition
Those elements which have a tendency to lose their valence electrons (electrons of the outermost orbit) and form a positive ion, are considered metals. Metals are reducing agents. Greater the tendency to lose electrons, stronger is the reducing agent.
Metallic Character Depends On
Atomic Size

Greater the atomic size, the farther the outermost orbit, and thus lesser the nuclear pull exerted on it. As a result, electron(s) can be removed more easily from the valence shell → more metallic.

Nuclear Charge

Greater the nuclear charge, greater is the force exerted by the nucleus on the electron(s) of the outermost orbit. This makes it difficult to remove electrons from the outermost orbit → metallic nature decreases.

Hydrogen Note: Hydrogen is a non-metal. It has only one electron and one proton. On losing this electron it forms a cation (H⁺) which has only one proton — therefore its cation is also called a proton.
Trends in Metallic Character
↓ Down a Group — Metallic Character INCREASES
Metallic Character — Down a Group↑ Increases

Reason: On moving down a group, the atomic size increases and the nuclear charge also increases. The effect of an increased atomic size is greater as compared to the increased nuclear charge. Therefore, metallic nature increases as one moves down a group (they can lose electrons easily).

Group 1 (least → most metallic): Li → Na → K → Rb → Cs

Note: Francium (Fr) is radioactive so its properties are not known.

→ Across a Period — Metallic Character DECREASES
Metallic Character — Across Period↓ Decreases

Reason: On moving across a period, nuclear pull increases due to the increase in atomic number, and thus the atomic size decreases. Hence, elements cannot lose electrons easily → metallic nature decreases.

2nd period: Li (most metallic) → … → F (non-metal)

3rd period: Na (most metallic) → … → Cl (non-metal)

3rd Period Example: Li | Be → Metals   |   B → Metalloid   |   C N O → Non-metals
3rd Period: Na | Mg | Al → Metals   |   Si → Metalloid   |   P | S | Cl → Non-metals

Periodic Property 3

Non-metallic Character

Elements with a tendency to gain electrons in order to attain octet in their outermost orbit are considered as non-metals.

Definition
Those elements, which have a tendency to gain electrons, in order to attain octet in their outermost orbit, are considered as non-metals. Non-metals are oxidising agents. Non-metals usually have 5, 6 or 7 electrons in their outermost orbits.
Non-metallic Character Depends On
Atomic Size

The smaller the atomic size, the greater is the nuclear pull and the tendency to gain electrons → more non-metallic by nature.

Nuclear Charge

The greater the nuclear charge, the greater is the tendency to gain electron(s) → more non-metallic is the element.

Trends in Non-metallic Character
↓ Down a Group — Non-metallic Character DECREASES
Non-metallic Character — Down Group↓ Decreases

Reason: The atomic size increases due to addition of new shells over successive periods. Though nuclear charge increases, the effect of increasing atomic size is greater. Therefore non-metallic nature decreases down the group.

Group 14 example:
C → Non-metal
Si → Metalloid
Ge → Metalloid
Sn → Metal
Pb → Metal
→ Across a Period — Non-metallic Character INCREASES
Non-metallic Character — Across Period↑ Increases

Reason: On moving across a period, tendency to gain electron(s) increases due to increase in nuclear pull and decrease in atomic size. Therefore non-metallic character increases across a period from left to right.

3rd Period:
Na < Mg < Al < Si < P < S < Cl
(Non-metallic character increases →)
Nature of Oxides — Periodicity

The nature of oxides also shows periodicity.

Across a period: The oxides of elements in a particular period show decreasing basic nature and finally become acidic.

Down a group: The basic nature of oxides of metals increases.
Chemical Reactivity Trends

Across a period: Reactivity first decreases (metals losing electrons becomes harder as you move right) then increases (non-metals gaining electrons becomes easier). Silicon is the least reactive element in the 3rd period.

Down a group: For metals, reactivity increases (losing electrons becomes easier). For non-metals, reactivity decreases (gaining electrons becomes harder). Most reactive metal: Cs. Most reactive non-metal: F (Fluorine, top of Group 17).

Periodic Property 4

Ionisation Potential / Ionisation Energy (I.E.)

The energy required to remove an electron from a neutral isolated gaseous atom.

Definition
The energy required to remove an electron from a neutral isolated gaseous atom and convert it into a positively charged gaseous ion is called ionisation potential (I.P.) or ionisation energy (I.E.) or first ionisation energy (IE₁).
Formula & Unit
M(g) + I.E. → M⁺(g) + e⁻ [M can be any element]
Unit: I.E. is measured in electron volts per atom (eV/atom) and its S.I. unit is kilojoule per mole (kJ mol⁻¹).
Note: Metals usually have low I.E. whereas non-metals have high I.E.
I.E. Depends On
Atomic Size

The greater the atomic size, the lesser the force of attraction between the nucleus and outermost electrons. Electrons of the outermost shell lie further away → removal easier → ionisation energy required is less.

Nuclear Charge

The greater the nuclear charge, greater is the attraction for the electrons of the outermost shell. The electrons are more firmly held → greater energy required → higher I.E.

Trends in Ionisation Energy
→ Across a Period — I.E. INCREASES (with exceptions)
Ionisation Energy — Across Period↑ Increases

Reason: Atomic size decreases due to an increase in nuclear charge → more energy required to remove electrons.

Periodic Property 5

Electron Affinity (E.A.) / Electron Gain Enthalpy

Energy released when an extra electron is added to a neutral isolated gaseous atom.

Definition
The amount of energy released while converting a neutral gaseous isolated atom into a negatively charged gaseous ion (anion) by the addition of an electron is called Electron Affinity (E.A.).
Formula & Unit
X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g) + E.A. [X is any element in gaseous state]

Example:  Cl(g) + e⁻ → Cl⁻(g) + 349 kJ/mol

Therefore, electron affinity of chlorine is –349 kJ/mol.

Unit: electron volts per atom (eV/atom) or kJ mol⁻¹.
Sign: Electron affinity is represented by a negative sign [–] e.g., E.A. of Cl = –349 kJ/mol.
E.A. Depends On
Atomic Size

The smaller the atomic size, the greater the electron affinity, because the effective attractive force between the nucleus and the valence electrons is greater in smaller atoms → electrons held firmly → greater tendency to attract incoming electron.

Nuclear Charge

The greater the nuclear charge, greater is the electron affinity, because increase in nuclear charge increases the effective attractive force on the valence electrons.

Trends in Electron Affinity
→ Across a Period — E.A. INCREASES
Electron Affinity — Across Period↑ Increases

Atomic size decreases and nuclear charge increases → highest E.A. for Group 17 (halogens), lowest for Group 1 (alkali metals).

↓ Down a Group — E.A. DECREASES
Electron Affinity — Down Group↓ Decreases

Atomic size increases more than nuclear charge → net decrease in effective attraction → E.A. decreases.

Periodic Property 6

Electronegativity (E.N.)

The tendency of an atom in a molecule to attract the shared pair of electrons towards itself.

Definition
The tendency of an atom in a molecule to attract the shared pair of electrons towards itself is called its electronegativity.
Key Facts About Electronegativity
Dimensionless Property
Electronegativity is a dimensionless property — it only indicates a tendency. It shows the net result of the tendencies of different elements to attract the bond-forming electron pair.
Pauling Scale (1932)
Devised by Linus Pauling. Highest value: Fluorine = 4.0. Lowest value: Caesium = 0.7. Most widely used scale of electronegativity.
General Rule: Metals show lower electronegativity as compared to non-metals. Thus metals are electropositive and non-metals are electronegative.
Diagonal Relationship: The elements of the second period show resemblance in properties with the elements of the next group of the third period, due to very similar electronegativity difference. This leads to a diagonal relationship: Li & Mg, Be & Al, B & Si. These elements are called bridge elements.
E.N. Depends On
Atomic Size

The greater the size of the atom, the lesser the electronegativity, since the electrons being farther away from the nucleus experience a lesser force of attraction.

Nuclear Charge

The greater the nuclear charge, the greater the electronegativity, because increase in nuclear charge causes electron attraction with a greater force.

Trends in Electronegativity
→ Across a Period — E.N. INCREASES
Electronegativity — Across Period↑ Increases

Nuclear charge increases due to increase in atomic number → electronegativity increases from left to right in a period. In 2nd period: Li (1.0) → Be (1.5) → … → F (4.0).

↓ Down a Group — E.N. DECREASES
Electronegativity — Down Group↓ Decreases

Atomic number increases, but due to addition of extra shells, atomic size increases. The effect of increasing atomic size overcomes the effect of increasing nuclear charge → E.N. decreases. Group 1: Li (1.0) → … → Fr (0.7).

Note: Greater the value of electron affinity, the easier it is to gain electrons and more non-metallic or more electronegative or more oxidising is the element.

Special Topic

Atomic Number & Mass Number

Relation between atomic number and mass number for light elements.

Atomic Number (Z)
The atomic number of an element is equal to the number of protons in the nucleus. Atomic number is the unique property of an element, because no two elements have the same atomic number.
Mass Number (A)
The mass number of an element is the sum of the number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of the atom of that element.
Mass Number (A) = No. of Protons (p) + No. of Neutrons (n)
Significance of Atomic Number (Z)
Unique Identity
No two elements have the same atomic number. It distinguishes each element from all others.
Electronic Configuration
Gives the electronic configuration. e.g., Z = 13 → config. 2, 8, 3 (Group 13, Period 3).
Position in Table
Helps find the element's group and period in the periodic table. Z = 17 → Period 3, Group 17.
Pattern in Light Elements
For light elements with an even number of protons (e.g., ⁴₂He, ¹²₆C, etc.), their mass numbers are twice the atomic numbers, except ⁹₄Be and ⁴⁰₁₈Ar.
For light elements with an odd number of protons (e.g., ⁷₃Li, ¹¹₅B, ¹⁹₉F, ²³₁₁Na), their mass numbers are twice the atomic numbers + 1 (A = 2Z + 1), except ¹⁴₇N and ¹₁H.
Elements with n/p (neutron/proton) ratio around 1 are stable, e.g., light metals like sodium, potassium, calcium. Elements with n/p ratio 1.5 and above are radioactive, e.g., heavy metals like uranium — they emit radiations and are unstable.

Alkali Metals vs Halogens

A detailed comparison of Group 1 (Alkali Metals) and Group 17 (Halogens).

Property Alkali Metals [Group 1] Halogens [Group 17]
ElementsLi, Na, K, Rb, Cs, FrF, Cl, Br, I, At
OccurrenceCombined state (reactive)Combined state as salts
Physical StateShining white solid metals; soft; Li is hardest; can be cut with a knife; become dull as they react with airNon-metals; diatomic in gaseous state; F (very reactive, poisonous yellow gas), Cl (poisonous yellow-green gas), Br (poisonous red-brown volatile liquid), I (dark grey crystalline solid)
Valence ElectronsPossess ONE valence electron; show similar propertiesPossess SEVEN valence electrons; show similar properties
ConductionGood conductors of electricityNon-conductors of electricity
NatureHighly reactive, electropositive metalsHighly reactive, electronegative non-metals
Metallic CharacterIncreases from Li to FrDecreases from F to I (non-metallic character decreases)
M.P. & B.P.Decreases down the groupIncreases down the group
Atomic SizeHave the largest atomic size in their period (except inert gases); increases down the groupHave the smallest atomic size in their period; increases down the group
Ionisation EnergyLowest I.E. in their period; decreases down the groupHigh I.E. (lower than noble gases) in their period; decreases down the group
Electron AffinityLow E.A. values; further decrease down the groupHigh E.A. values; they too decrease down the group
ElectronegativityLowest E.N. in their period; decreases down the groupHighest E.N. in their period; decreases down the group

Quick Memory Aid — At a Glance
Moving LEFT → RIGHT across a period
↑ Increases: Valence electrons, Ionisation energy, Non-metallic character, Electron affinity, Electronegativity, Oxidising nature, Atomic No.

↓ Decreases: Atomic size, Metallic character, Reducing nature, Basic nature of oxides
Moving TOP → BOTTOM down a group
↑ Increases: Atomic size, Metallic character, Reducing nature, Basic nature of oxides, Density, Atomic No.

↓ Decreases: Ionisation energy, Non-metallic character, Electron affinity, Electronegativity, Oxidising nature

= Remains Same: Valence electrons, Valency, Number of valence electrons
